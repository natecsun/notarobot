Three:

In the west, whatever that means in this land, is a forest of immeasurable depth and phosphorescent foliage, it's trees bearing the most delectable and bitter of fruits, the most pompous and humble of flowers. The forest provides habitat to no beast, neither vicious, gentle, comical, nor fearsome, though it is said that perhaps there were once many creatures there who were merely drawn to the sky long ago to investigate its intentions. When the wind blows, the trees become giddy with excitement and may display their delight by emitting deep-blue beams of light from the ends of their branches. The great explorer Auraleus Branson first traveled to this land in search of a supposed treasure which its forest held, though the only clue he had obtained regarding the treasure's whereabouts was, "it will surround you." At first, Auraleus had thought that the treasure might have something to do with the multi-colored flows pumping through wiry veins in the trees' interiors, a swirl of pastels made visible through transparent bark. He never found out for sure; in addition to being transparent, the bark also proved quite impenetrable. Auraleus never obtained whatever it was he might have seeked in the forest.

Two:

In the south, or what Auraleus determined to be south, is a network of seas wherein gravity is so suspended as to leave waves frozen in their place once they have formed, though remaining pliable should some suggestive force push them again into lurching motion. However, suggestive forces were hard to come by in this region, as were any beings with an interest in aquatics. Such a desolation is perhaps natural in a body of liquid which retains the mark of a hand print and leaves tunnels in the wake of any object that passes through. Swimming or otherwise navigating through such an environment is nearly impossible, though Auraleus Branson was willing to give it a shot on his second journey to the land, in which he had been in pursuit of a legendary anchor which was told to have fallen from the only vessel ever to have somehow held its weight upon the engulfing waters. Auraleus could not imagine any vessel that would've been able to perform such a task on such a surface. The idea that this apparent impossibility could ever have been contradicted drove the explorer to risk his life in seeking a remnant of the phantom ship. The first inclination Auraleus had while maneuvering about in his aqua-pod-a vessel specially designed by his team of engineers to defy the properties of this sculptured sea-was to seek out any hollows in the water which reached to the floor. However, such formations were not to be found and Auraleus never encountered the trophy which had been spoken of in so many taverns with hushed tones. Soon afterward, the strength of the legend began to fade.

One:

Along the north and east of the land lies one edge of an expansive canyon, the other side of which is visible only with telescopic aid from the vantage point of the field which serves as the heart of the canyon, forest, and seas. It was from this area that Auraleus had made his directional determinations, and it was from this gray grass field that Auraleus had, on his third trip to the land, made an attempt to cross the black sink of the canyon. "Surely," he thought then, "there are spoils to be had on the other side." Previous explorers had tried and failed because the mechanics of their flying vessels always mysteriously malfunctioned after traveling just a few feet over the gap. Auraleus knew that his method should lack machinery, and it occurred to him to use grassblades of varying length in the field to make himself a set of wings. Once the wings were completed, Auraleus-the possesor of a nimble and highly flexible body-stitched them painlessly into his shoulder blades and had his engineers build a catapulting device for setting him airborne. The device ejected him without a hitch, and Auraleus experienced a nervous exhiliration due to the fact that there had been no chance for a test flight of the wings. Luckily, they held him aloft and on course as expected; until, that is, he had traveled several hundred feet over the canyon and a highly intent gust of wind blasted him smoothly and swiftly back in the direction from which he'd come. This event became routine on Auraleus' dozens of subsequent attempts, and after a great period of time he finally gave up crossing the canyon. It had never occurred to Auraleus to simply hitch a ride with one of the clouds that were in perpetual motion along a narrow belt from the sky above the gray grass field, across the canyon, and to the other side and beyond, in spite of the rope-like wisps which dangled lithely from them.

Ignition:

There are no mirrors in this land, but strewn about the gray grass field are numerous sizable shards of reflective glass from various vessels that in some way met their doom while carelessly surveying the area. It was in such a fractured slice of pod-bay glass that Auraleus, now heavy with the weight of graying hair and sagging skin, observed his hand as he folded it into the shape of a crude photon accelerator and hoped with all of his might that it would become real. He thought of the times that being able to make this happen would've come in handy and chuckled softly as his hand fell limp. Auraleus had just completed his fourth and what he knew to be final mission in this sector, and as he waited patiently for his crew to retrieve him he could do nothing but contemplate what had come upon him in the Castle Made of Rocketships. It rests in the northwest corner of the gray grass field, approximately one mile east of the forest and one mile south of the canyon's edge.

The castle is anchored by five lofty towers positioned such that, as connected by the castle walls, they are the vertices of a vast pentagon; as connected by the five-line network of platforms running from tower-top to tower-top, they are the points on a terrestrial star. The entire body of the castle-it's towers, walls, and platforms-consists of polished, pristine remnants of ancient rocketships which seem to have been cut and formed to fit together like interstellar puzzle pieces. Engines, body fragments, and interior elements are all exposed and somehow coalesced into the shape of a five-sided castle, though no whole rocketship remains intact (nearly so in the towers, however). There seemed to be no outer entrance along any of the walls, and Auraleus' attempts to dig a tunnel to the castle's interior were thwarted: it's foundation ran deeper than he had imagined it could. However, much to his surprise, his surveillance indicated that the top of the castle was uncovered aside from the network of platforms. Auraleus still sported the wings that he had used in his third mission to the land, and the catapult from that same mission remained where he'd left it. Though his wings had slightly withered over time, Auraleus determined that they should still serve him well as they had on many occasions since he attached them, and of course any device constructed by his engineers took many more ages to deteriorate than he could hope to live.

Sure enough, the wings still worked wonderfully, and after initiating the catapult Auraleus glided gently through the sky in a wide vertical arc before finally landing himself in the center of the castle's interior. Auraleus immediately surmised that he was in what might be considered the courtyard of castle, and he also immediately surmised that he was disappointed. There was no sign of the treasure he had heard so many tales of, or any treasure at all. Aside from the one and a half dozen uniformly chiseled shards of machinery that projected from the ground in three rows in a small square of the courtyard to one side, it seemed that the area was little more than a flat, barren five-walled stretch of hard-packed violet dirt, no portion of which would allow itself to be penetrated by any of Auraleus' digging equipment. After some time, and after realizing that he had not planned a way to get out of the castle, Auraleus watched as a rectangular segment of the wall that hadn't looked door-like at all opened up and a vaguely smiling old man with short white hair and sandstone eyes wearing a mild-blue jumpsuit emerged and walked up to him intently. When he was about five feet in front of Auraleus, the man stopped and without looking away from the explorer reached into a knapsack that was strapped to his back and removed a small cube of mesh and metal tubing which he proceeded to unfold. It was a trampoline. He reached again into the knapsack and removed a thin manuscript. Still silent, he walked up to Auraleus, handed him the manuscript, turned around, and walked into the non-door again without looking back. Once it closed, Auraleus had almost forgotten that it ever existed.

The aging explorer could hardly fathom that his expeditions in this land had rewarded him with no spoils, no goods for trade, no trophies. There was the manuscript, but his interest in it was minute at best. Even those who might have reason to find the information therein intriguing probably wouldn't believe it at all. He saw his crew approaching over the horizon to the west and perked up. Aurleus took one last good look at this land, at the forest, at the seas in the distance, at the canyon, at the thin isolated stream of clouds, and finally at the proud, shimmering Castle Made of Rocketships, which almost seemed to mock him. He felt simultaneously inclined to curse the land under his breath and simply shake his head in awe. For the fourth time in his career he would leave this land with empty hands and a heavy heart, though the next time he was on Earth, Auraleus Branson could tell the people that he knew what became of their astronauts upon those rare occasions that they were irretrievably stranded in the void. He didn't know if he would.

BIOGRAPHY
-By Bill Sutherland (AP)

Formed in 1991, Toy Musclecar's original lineup featured guitarists Karl Kaemingk, John Kooiman, bassist Elmer Hopper, and drummer Lou Arkema. Despite being nearly broke and having no commercial support, they took over the underground in just a couple of years thanks to brilliant songwriting, heartfelt & sincere lyrics, and a unique blend of dark ambience and sugar coated candy pop.

Following their debut album hot air balloons, the band generated an underground buzz thanks to the fluke hit single semi trucks (a compelling, melodic take on modern love) which received some airplay on college radio stations nationwide. With relentless touring and energetic live shows, the band turned themselves into underground cult icons. However, tragedy struck in early 1992. The night before a sold-out show was to be played in Cincinnati, guitarist John Kooiman was found dead in his hotel room from a heroin overdose. After a yearlong merry-go-round of guitarists (including Norm Van Steenwyke - now the front man for the anti-pop outfit Violent Joe), the band finally came into contact with Ritchie Victoria, an unknown out of Indianapolis. In mid 1993, they released a new album entitled black marker, dull sentence, which became a massive underground hit. The album was much more coherent as a whole - seamlessly blending Victoria's darker tone with the spaced out vibe of the band, all the while drawing from the pain of losing their former guitarist.

Black Marker has been described as the band's masterpiece & even as one of the best albums of the decade. The entire album flows together magically - screaming melody and denial vs. pain and anger.

Sadly, just 3 months after black marker hit the shelves, Ritchie Victoria committed suicide in his Indianapolis home.

The rest of the band tried to regroup, and even released a new album - generation super X (with newly recruited guitarist Reggie Regnerus). Despite moderate success, it lacked Victoria's signature sound and as a whole the album seemed scattered and incoherent. Just a few months after the release of generation super x, the band broke up for good.

BIOGRAPHY
-By Bill Sutherland (AP)

Brilliant songwriter Ritchie Victoria (of Toy Musclecar fame) also released a side project just a month before his death. Using the moniker Bicycles That Are Dead, he toys with pseudo minimal electonica. This album cemented his legacy as a musical genius. Surprisingly quiet and melodic, yet still his dark mood is still in tact. Although the album only included 7 tracks, it weighed in over an hour long, thanks in part to the brilliant 24 minute closer please take the sunglasses back.

The album starts off with staring at the wall with no smile and no name which, despite it's depressing title, is quite calm and soothing. Almost bringing back memories of early classic pianists. Next, the pounding beats and rhythmic feedback of my machine gun's name is jennifer, safe to say the angriest song found here - he gives us this piece filled with denial and angst. Yet midway through we find out that even Victoria's angriest moments turn into disoriented reverb and melodies as he switches gears and fades out the drums, leaving us with nothing but gruesome background sound. Tape recorded voices and atmospheric surf guitar lead us into when i was younger, i had fun. Starting off slowly and leading us into a world filled with insanity, somehow the gentle surf guitars turn into reverberated noise only to yield it's way to brash distortion and slightly off key vocal harmonies - all in seamless transition. All my friends who live far away takes this note and runs with it even more, giving us the same type of echoed offkey harmonies and distortion mixed with a soothing background of guitar and pounding beats. Sensing we need a break, he gives us we left the baby in the ocean - a 57 second interlude of haunting beats and guitar samples. Despite the short length, Victoria actually had a few friends come in and make cameos for this track. It's safe to say the end result is far from what they delivered to him. a long drive home (in a red car) is by far the quietest piece found here - the drums echo as if induced by sleep, while the background is filled with luscious guitarscape and delay. The album closer adds a perfect completion to this incredible album. please take the sunglasses back is Victoria at his most sincere. Meandering around playfully for nearly 5 minutes, we finally enter the song only to find ourselves in a haunting, sick, dark place - surely a microcosm of his mind. A deliberate, hypnotizing pace drones ad infinitum. Midway through the song, after what seems like hours of slow tortured death, he finally works his way up to a melodramatic climax. A beautifully subtle and simplistic guitar solo adds the perfect finishing touch to this masterpiece. Victoria's music didn't use proper measures or time signatures, he just made his own rules and it worked - Beautifully. A brilliant album made by one of the better musicians of the last decade.
